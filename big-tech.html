Amazon — 16 Leadership Principles

Know every LP with an example story ready — interviewers pick 2-4 per round

ℹ️
How Amazon interviews work
Each interviewer is assigned 1-3 LPs to probe. They'll ask a behavioural question and dig with "tell me more", "what was your specific contribution", "what would you do differently". Prepare 2 stories per LP. Every story must follow STAR: Situation → Task → Action → Result with numbers.
1
Customer Obsession
Leaders start with the customer and work backwards. They work vigorously to earn and keep customer trust.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you went above and beyond for a customer."

Keywords:

Sacrificed speed/cost for customer benefit, advocated for users against internal pressure, fixed a customer problem that wasn't your direct responsibility.

Red flags:

Stories where you prioritised process over the customer outcome. Never say "it wasn't my job."

2
Ownership
Leaders are owners. They think long-term and don't sacrifice long-term value for short-term results. They never say "that's not my job."
Question:

"Describe a time you took on a project that was outside your job description."

Keywords:

Stepped up when no one else did, fixed problems in adjacent teams' code, stayed late to resolve a production incident even though it was someone else's service.

Tip:

Show the business impact, not just "I helped". What would have happened if you hadn't stepped in?

3
Invent and Simplify
Leaders expect and require innovation and invention from their teams and always find ways to simplify.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you innovated on behalf of customers or simplified a complex process."

Story angle:

Replaced a complex multi-step manual process with automation. Redesigned an API that reduced client code complexity. Proposed a simpler architecture that reduced bug rate. Quantify: "reduced deploy time from 2 hours to 10 minutes."

4
Are Right, A Lot
Leaders are right a lot. They have strong judgment and good instincts. They seek diverse perspectives and work to disconfirm their beliefs.
Question:

"Tell me about a time your judgment turned out to be correct despite opposition."

Also:

"Tell me about a time you were wrong." — Being wrong AND adapting quickly scores very well. Never claim you've never been wrong.

5
Learn and Be Curious
Leaders are never done learning and always seek to improve themselves. They are curious about new possibilities.
Question:

"What have you learned in the past year? How did you apply it?"

Story angle:

Self-taught a new technology (Kafka, K8s), read papers and applied research, experimented with side projects, upskilled after a production incident revealed a gap.

6
Hire and Develop the Best
Leaders raise the performance bar with every hire and promotion. They recognize exceptional talent and willingly move them throughout the organization.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you helped a teammate grow or mentored someone."

Story angle:

Pair-programmed regularly, gave detailed code review feedback, helped someone get promoted, identified skills gap and proposed training, coached a junior engineer through a difficult project.

7
Insist on the Highest Standards
Leaders have relentlessly high standards — many people may think these standards are unreasonably high. They continually raise the bar.
Question:

"Describe a time you refused to accept a lower standard than what was needed."

Story angle:

Pushed back on releasing code with known bugs, wrote tests when team culture didn't require them, rejected a design that would create tech debt, added monitoring when no one asked for it.

8
Think Big
Thinking small is a self-fulfilling prophecy. Leaders create and communicate a bold direction that inspires results.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you proposed a bold idea or a direction that changed the approach."

Story angle:

Proposed migrating from monolith to microservices when team was thinking "just refactor this class", suggested a new product feature that opened a new market segment, architected for 10x scale when only 2x was requested.

9
Bias for Action
Speed matters in business. Many decisions and actions are reversible and do not need extensive study. We value calculated risk-taking.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you made an important decision without complete information."

Story angle:

Deployed a hotfix during an incident without a full root cause analysis, ran a quick prototype to validate an idea before writing a full spec, made a reversible architectural decision quickly to unblock the team.

Key phrase:

"I knew I had enough data to act. Waiting would have cost more than being wrong and adjusting."

10
Frugality
Accomplish more with less. Constraints breed resourcefulness, self-sufficiency, and invention. There are no extra points for growing headcount, budget, or fixed expense.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you achieved a goal with fewer resources than expected."

Story angle:

Used open-source instead of buying a $50K tool, reduced cloud costs by 40% through optimisation, built a feature with 2 engineers that others quoted would need 5.

11
Earn Trust
Leaders listen attentively, speak candidly, and treat others respectfully. They are vocally self-critical, even when it is awkward or embarrassing.
Question:

"Describe a time you had to deliver difficult feedback or disagree with a colleague."

Story angle:

Told a senior engineer their design had a flaw (respectfully, with data), admitted a mistake to a stakeholder before they noticed, gave honest performance feedback that was hard to hear.

12
Dive Deep
Leaders operate at all levels, stay connected to the details, and audit frequently. No task is beneath them.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you identified a root cause that others had missed."

Story angle:

Profiled production to find a 10x performance bottleneck no one had found, read the Kafka source code to understand an obscure behavior, spent a day with production logs to find intermittent data corruption.

Key phrase:

"I didn't accept 'it works in prod' as evidence. I went into the data and found..."

13
Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit
Leaders are obligated to respectfully challenge decisions when they disagree, even when doing so is uncomfortable. But once a decision is made, they commit wholly.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager or team and how you handled it."

Structure:

1) I clearly stated my position with evidence. 2) I listened to counter-arguments. 3) The decision went against me. 4) I committed fully and helped make it succeed. 5) Result (even if I wasn't right).

Never say:

"I gave up because they outranked me" or "I sabotaged the decision after I lost."

14
Deliver Results
Leaders focus on the key inputs for their business and deliver them with the right quality and in a timely fashion. Despite setbacks, they rise to the occasion and never settle.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you delivered a project under a difficult constraint."

Story angle:

Met a hard deadline despite scope changes, recovered a failing project and shipped on time, delivered while a key team member was absent.

Always include:

Specific metrics. "Delivered 2 weeks early" or "reduced error rate from 5% to 0.1%". No numbers = weak story.

15
Strive to be Earth's Best Employer
Leaders work every day to create a safer, more productive, higher performing, more diverse, and more just work environment.
Question:

"How have you created an inclusive environment or supported teammate wellbeing?"

Story angle:

Pushed back on crunch culture, advocated for a colleague's promotion, introduced on-call rotation to spread incident burden equitably, ensured quieter voices were heard in design discussions.

16
Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility
Amazon has an outsized impact. Leaders create more than they take and always leave things better than how they found them.
Question:

"Tell me about a time you considered the broader impact of your technical decisions."

Story angle:

Reduced cloud footprint/carbon impact, audited a feature for bias or privacy implications, open-sourced internal tooling, documented a process so the team after you could build on it.

Amazon — Behavioral Q&A

Common questions with full STAR answer frameworks

1
Walk me through the most complex technical project you've worked on.
Hard▼

This tests: Dive Deep, Deliver Results, Think Big, Invent and Simplify.

Framework:

  • S: Set the scale — how many users, how much data, what business impact
  • T: What was your specific responsibility (avoid "we")
  • A: Walk through your specific decisions — what alternatives you considered and why you chose this approach
  • R: Concrete numbers — latency improvement, cost reduction, reliability gain, revenue impact
💡
Do
Pick a project where YOU made a consequential architectural or engineering decision. Talk about trade-offs you evaluated. Mention a mistake you made and how you corrected it — this shows Dive Deep + Earn Trust.
🚫
Avoid
"We built X and it was great." No individual ownership. "The team decided." No demonstrated judgment. Projects without measurable outcomes.
2
Tell me about a time you had to deal with a significant technical debt problem.
Medium▼

This tests: Insist on Highest Standards, Ownership, Deliver Results.

S:

Our payment service had grown organically over 4 years. It had no unit tests, mixed SQL directly in controllers, and any change caused unpredictable failures. Deployment took 2 hours and required manual steps.

T:

I was tasked with adding a new payment method. I could hack it in a week or advocate for fixing the foundation first.

A:

I wrote a proposal quantifying the cost: 3 production incidents in 6 months, each averaging 4h × 3 engineers = 72 engineer-hours. I modeled that 3 weeks of refactoring would recover that cost in 4 months. Got approval. I split the work: extracted repository layer (no behaviour change), added tests for critical paths, automated deployment. Delivered the new payment method in week 4 with full test coverage.

R:

Deployment time dropped from 2h to 12 minutes. Zero production incidents over the next 6 months. Feature velocity in that service increased 40% as measured by PRs merged per sprint.

3
Describe a situation where you had to work with a difficult colleague or stakeholder.
Medium▼

This tests: Earn Trust, Have Backbone, Deliver Results.

Key principles:

  • Never badmouth the person — focus on the professional disagreement, not their character
  • Show you took responsibility for the relationship dynamic too
  • Show the outcome was a win for both parties
What to say:

"We had different priorities — they were optimising for delivery speed, I was concerned about reliability. I asked for a 30-minute meeting where I brought data: our last 3 major incidents were all caused by rushing. We agreed on a shared definition of 'done' that included automated tests and a canary deploy. The next release went out on time and with zero post-deploy incidents."

4
Tell me about a time you failed. What did you learn?
Hard▼

This tests: Earn Trust (self-critical), Are Right A Lot (learn from mistakes), Learn and Be Curious.

This is one of the most important questions. Amazon explicitly wants people who are vocally self-critical. A weak or deflecting answer kills your chances.

💡
Formula
Pick a real, significant failure (not "I worked too hard"). Explain what you did wrong specifically. Show what you learned and how you changed your behavior. Show the durable improvement. A candidate who's never failed is not credible; a candidate who failed and grew is exactly what Amazon wants.
Example:

"I shipped a database migration without testing it on a dataset representative of production. The migration ran fine on 100K rows in staging but timed out on 50M rows in prod, causing a 90-minute outage. I failed to Dive Deep into the data distribution. After that, I made it a personal standard to always run migrations against a production-sized data sample in pre-prod. I also introduced a migration checklist that the team now uses on every release."

5
How do you prioritise when you have multiple urgent projects competing for your time?
Medium▼

This tests: Deliver Results, Bias for Action, Customer Obsession.

Framework to describe:

  1. Identify customer impact of each item — which delays hurt users most?
  2. Identify reversibility — what's harder to undo? Do that first
  3. Check dependencies — is anyone else blocked by me? Unblock them first
  4. Communicate clearly — tell stakeholders of the others what to expect
  5. Time-box ruthlessly — use fixed time slots, don't let one project expand

Then give a specific example from your experience where you used this approach.

Amazon — Technical Questions

Java/backend-focused questions Amazon asks senior engineers

6
Design Amazon's order processing system. Handle 1M orders/day with payment, inventory, and notification.
Hard▼

Clarify first: Average or peak? Global or one region? Consistency requirements for inventory? SLA for order confirmation?

High-level design:

  1. API Gateway → Order Service: validates request, creates order in PENDING state, publishes OrderCreated event via Kafka (Outbox pattern)
  2. Payment Service: consumes event, charges card, publishes PaymentCompleted or PaymentFailed
  3. Inventory Service: consumes PaymentCompleted, reserves stock, publishes InventoryReserved
  4. Notification Service: consumes events, sends email/SMS/push
  5. Saga orchestrator: watches all events; on failure, triggers compensating transactions (refund, release reservation)

Data: PostgreSQL per service (service-owns-data), Redis for session/idempotency keys, Kafka for event bus.

💡
Amazon expects
Operational excellence thinking: how do you monitor each service? What's your on-call runbook? How do you handle a payment service brownout? Show you think about failure modes, not just happy paths.
7
How would you design a distributed rate limiter that works across multiple data centers?
Hard▼

Single-node rate limiter (in-memory or single Redis) breaks with horizontal scaling. Distributed options:

  • Redis cluster with Lua script — atomic INCR + EXPIRE in one script; Redis handles replication. Latency ~1ms. Works well for ≤10 data centers
  • Token bucket in Redis — store token count + last refill time; Lua script refills proportionally to time elapsed and deducts. Allows bursting
  • Approximate local counting + sync — each node keeps local counter; every 100ms syncs delta to Redis. Allows small overcount in exchange for much lower Redis load (Google's approach with Doorman)
  • Sliding window in Redis with sorted set — ZADD request timestamps, ZREMRANGEBYSCORE to remove old entries, ZCARD to count. Accurate but O(N) per request
💡
Trade-off to mention
Cross-DC synchronous rate limiting adds latency for every request. Most production systems accept 5-10% overage and use local counting with eventual sync — the cost of occasional overage is less than the latency cost of perfect coordination.
8
Explain how you'd ensure zero-downtime deployments in a Java microservice on AWS.
Hard▼
  • Blue-Green or Rolling deploy — Kubernetes rolling update with maxUnavailable: 0
  • Health probes — readiness probe ensures traffic only goes to healthy pods
  • Database migrations — always backwards-compatible: add columns (nullable), don't rename/remove in same deploy. Use Flyway with expand/contract pattern
  • API versioning — keep v1 and v2 endpoints running concurrently during transition
  • Connection draining — ALB connection draining gives in-flight requests time to complete before pod is terminated. Set terminationGracePeriodSeconds appropriately
  • Feature flags — deploy code dark (off), test in prod, ramp traffic gradually, instant rollback by toggling flag
  • Circuit breaker — if new version has elevated error rate, circuit opens and routes to old version
9
What's your approach to debugging a production memory leak in a Java application?
Hard▼

This tests: Dive Deep, Deliver Results, Ownership.

  1. Confirm it's a leak — heap used grows over time even after GC; OOMKilled events in K8s logs
  2. Capture a heap dump: jcmd <pid> GC.heap_dump /tmp/heap.hprof or wait for OOM (-XX:+HeapDumpOnOutOfMemoryError)
  3. Analyse with Eclipse MAT or VisualVM — "Dominator Tree" shows which objects hold most memory; "Leak Suspects" report often finds the culprit
  4. Common culprits: static collections not cleared, listeners registered but never removed, ThreadLocal not removed after request, unbounded cache (Caffeine with no eviction), large byte[] held in session
  5. Fix + verify — add metrics: jvm_memory_used_bytes in Prometheus/Grafana; deploy fix and watch the sawtooth pattern disappear
10
How do you design for idempotency in a REST API? Why does it matter?
Medium▼

Idempotency ensures that repeating the same request produces the same result — critical for retries (network errors, timeouts, client retries).

Java — Idempotency key pattern
// Client sends Idempotency-Key header on POST
// POST /api/payments  Idempotency-Key: client-generated-uuid

@PostMapping("/payments")
public ResponseEntity<PaymentResponse> createPayment(
        @RequestHeader("Idempotency-Key") String idempotencyKey,
        @RequestBody PaymentRequest req) {

    // Check if we've already processed this key
    return idempotencyStore.get(idempotencyKey)
        .map(cached -> ResponseEntity.ok(cached))   // return cached result
        .orElseGet(() -> {
            PaymentResponse result = paymentService.process(req);
            idempotencyStore.store(idempotencyKey, result, Duration.ofHours(24));
            return ResponseEntity.status(201).body(result);
        });
}
// Store: Redis with TTL. Key must be scoped per customer to prevent cross-customer replay

HTTP methods: GET, PUT, DELETE are naturally idempotent. POST is not — use idempotency keys. PATCH may or may not be (depends on operation: "set to X" vs "add N").

Googleyness, cognitive ability, leadership, role-related knowledge

ℹ️
Google's 4 interview dimensions
Google scores every interview on: General Cognitive Ability (structured problem solving, learning from ambiguity), Leadership (emergent leadership, not just management), Googleyness (culture fit, intellectual humility, comfort with ambiguity), and Role-Related Knowledge (your specific technical domain).
🧠

Intellectual Humility

Say "I don't know but here's how I'd find out". Google values curiosity over pretending to know.

🤝

Collaborative

Show you build on others' ideas, give credit, pull people in rather than working alone by default.

📊

Data-Driven

Back claims with metrics, experiments, A/B tests. Opinions are hypotheses to be tested.

🔄

Comfort with Ambiguity

Show you can make progress when requirements are unclear — define the problem, then solve it.

Google — Behavioral Q&A

Googleyness and leadership questions with answer frameworks

11
Tell me about a time you influenced a technical decision without having formal authority.
Hard▼

This tests: Emergent leadership, collaboration, cognitive ability.

Google wants to see: You built a coalition through evidence and persuasion, not through hierarchy. You brought others along rather than going around them.

Framework:

1) Identify the problem and why it mattered. 2) Gather data / build prototype. 3) Find allies (who also cared?). 4) Present options with trade-offs, not a single mandate. 5) Let the team own the decision. 6) Result.

Key phrase:

"I didn't ask for permission — I built a working prototype over a weekend and shared the results. The numbers did the persuading."

12
Describe a time you had to learn something completely new under time pressure.
Medium▼

This tests: General cognitive ability (learning speed), Googleyness.

What to show:

  • How you approached the learning (official docs → tutorials → hands-on → expert consultation)
  • How you managed uncertainty ("I didn't know what I didn't know, so I first mapped out what questions I needed to answer")
  • The meta-skill: how you learn, not just what you learned
💡
Strong angle
Kubernetes, Kafka, a new cloud provider, a new programming language — pick the one where you went deepest fastest and had measurable impact within weeks. Explain your learning strategy, not just the outcome.
13
How do you handle disagreement in a design review? Give an example.
Medium▼

This tests: Collaboration, intellectual humility, leadership.

Google's pattern: Document the disagreement, discuss the trade-offs explicitly, run an experiment or prototype if the decision is reversible, make a time-bound decision if it's not.

Example:

"In a design review for our new caching layer, two engineers disagreed on Redis vs Memcached. Instead of debating, I suggested we write the trade-off matrix together: consistency model, cluster management, data types, client library maturity. Once we had it documented, the decision became obvious for our use case. The team aligned in 10 minutes."

Key phrase:

"I find that most engineering disagreements dissolve when you write down the constraints. People argue past each other because they're optimizing for different things."

14
Tell me about a time you simplified something that others thought was inherently complex.
Medium▼

This tests: Cognitive ability, engineering judgment, Googleyness.

Good examples for full-stack engineers:

  • Replaced a custom event system with standard Java ApplicationEvents — same behaviour, 80% less code
  • Replaced 6 microservices that had grown too small with 2 better-bounded services
  • Eliminated a complex Angular state management layer by using signals and AsyncPipe instead of NgRx for a feature that didn't warrant the complexity
  • Replaced an elaborate Helm chart with a simple Helm chart + Kustomize overlays that any engineer could understand
15
Why do you want to work at Google? (How to answer this authentically)
Easy▼

Don't say: "Great compensation", "amazing culture", "I've always dreamed of Google."

Do say something specific:

  • A specific Google technology or team that aligns with your expertise: "I've been using Cloud Spanner in my work and want to work on problems at that scale"
  • A specific product you're passionate about and what you'd improve

Research

Google — Technical Questions

System design and Java questions Google asks senior backend engineers

16
Design Google Maps' real-time traffic layer. How do you aggregate data from millions of devices?
Hard▼

Clarify: How often do devices report? What's the update latency requirement? What resolution (per-road segment vs per-intersection)?

Data pipeline:

  1. Device → Kafka: 500M devices × 1 report/30s = ~17M events/sec. Kafka partitioned by geo-hash
  2. Stream processor (Flink/Kafka Streams): aggregate speed data by road segment per 30s window; compute average speed, detect anomalies (accident = sudden speed drop)
  3. Storage: Redis for current traffic (fast read, TTL 60s), BigTable for historical patterns (ML training), PostgreSQL with PostGIS for road network
  4. Client delivery: WebSocket or Server-Sent Events for real-time updates; delta compression (only changed segments)
💡
Google expectsThey want to see you reason about data consistency trade-offs. Traffic data can be eventually consistent — a 30-second stale reading is acceptable. You don't need distributed transactions here. Also: how do you handle device privacy? Aggregate before storing, never store individual GPS traces.
17
How would you design a globally consistent distributed key-value store (like Spanner/Bigtable)?
Hard▼

This is asking you to reason about distributed systems fundamentals that underpin Cloud Spanner.

Core challenges:

  • Consistency across regions — Spanner uses TrueTime API (atomic clocks + GPS) for globally ordered timestamps. At Google, this is a hardware advantage. For us: hybrid logical clocks or vector clocks
  • Replication — Paxos/Raft per shard; Spanner uses Paxos groups (typically 5 replicas across 3+ regions)
  • Transactions — Two-phase locking within a shard; two-phase commit across shards (coordinator node per transaction)
  • Reads without coordination — stale reads can be served from any replica; strong reads go through the leader

Trade-off to mention: Global strong consistency costs ~100ms cross-region RTT. Most reads should use bounded staleness reads (10s-30s stale) for ~5ms local latency.

18
How do you approach code reviews? What makes a great code review comment?
Medium▼

This tests: Collaboration, engineering standards, leadership.

Google's code review culture is very specific:

  • Every change must be approved before merge — no exceptions
  • Review within 24 hours (not letting PRs rot)
  • Comments are labelled: nit: (minor style, can ignore), optional: (suggestion), or blocking
  • Focus on: correctness, readability, test coverage, performance implications, security

Great review comment structure: "What the problem is" → "Why it matters" → "Suggested fix or alternative". Never just "this is wrong."

💡
Google-specific
Mention you've read or agree with Google's Engineering Practices guide (it's public). They value reviewers who explain the "why" behind feedback, not just the "what". Also: separate correctness from style — correctness is blocking; style is a nit.
19
How do you write production-ready code? What checklist do you use before submitting a PR?
Medium▼

This tests: Insist on Highest Standards, Role-Related Knowledge.

My production-readiness checklist:

  • ✅ Unit tests with meaningful assertions (not just coverage %)
  • ✅ Integration test for the happy path + one error case
  • ✅ Error cases handled: what happens at each external call failure?
  • ✅ Logging: enough to diagnose issues without exposing PII
  • ✅ Metrics: new Prometheus counters/gauges for meaningful operations
  • ✅ Alerting: will we know if this breaks in prod?
  • ✅ Documentation: complex logic explained inline; public API documented
  • ✅ Database migrations: backwards-compatible; tested on prod-sized data
  • ✅ Security: no secrets in code, SQL parameterised, input validated
  • ✅ Performance: no N+1 queries, no blocking calls in hot path
20
Walk me through how you'd investigate a sudden 10x increase in API latency.
Hard▼

This tests: Systematic debugging, Dive Deep, Deliver Results.

Systematic investigation — don't guess, measure:

  1. Scope — is it all endpoints or one? All users or a subset? All regions or one?
  2. Timeline — when did it start? Correlate with deploys, traffic spikes, external events (certificate rotation, dependency updates)
  3. Metrics — check p50 vs p99: if p50 is fine but p99 is high = tail latency issue (GC pause, connection pool exhaustion). If both high = systemic issue
  4. Trace — pull distributed traces for slow requests. Which span is slow? DB query? External HTTP call? Kafka consumer lag?
  5. Resource check — CPU, memory, network I/O, disk I/O on all services in the path. Connection pool utilisation
  6. Dependency check — is a downstream service slow? Check its latency metrics
  7. DB — check slow query log, lock waits, connection pool exhaustion, table bloat (VACUUM not running?)
  8. Mitigation — can you rollback? Scale horizontally? Circuit break the slow dependency?
💡
Key phrase
"I narrow the problem before proposing solutions. I'd rather spend 10 minutes on data than 2 hours fixing the wrong thing."
